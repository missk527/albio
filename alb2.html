AL Biology 2. Carbohydrates: Monosaccharides and Disaccharides

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:24 | Introduction to Carbohydrates
  • This video explains carbohydrates, with a focus on monosaccharides and disaccharides.
  • It introduces what carbohydrates are and how simple carbohydrates are classified.
  • The video also explains how disaccharides are formed and broken down.
0:24 - 0:58 | What Carbohydrates Are
  • Carbohydrates are biological molecules made only from carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen.
  • The word carbohydrate gives clues about their composition: carbo means carbon, and hydrate refers to hydrogen and oxygen.
  • Hydrogen and oxygen are usually present in a 2:1 ratio, similar to water.
  • Carbohydrates can be represented by the general formula Cx(H2O)y.
0:58 - 1:55 | Functions of Carbohydrates
  • Simple carbohydrates such as glucose provide energy directly to cells during respiration.
  • Complex carbohydrates store energy, such as starch in plants and glycogen in animals.
  • Some carbohydrates form structural materials, including cellulose in plant cell walls and chitin in insect exoskeletons.
  • Carbohydrates also help with cell recognition through glycoproteins.
  • Simple sugars such as ribose and deoxyribose are used to build nucleic acids like RNA and DNA.
1:55 - 2:20 | Main Types of Carbohydrates
  • The three main types of carbohydrates are monosaccharides, disaccharides, and polysaccharides.
  • This video focuses on monosaccharides and disaccharides.
  • Monosaccharides and disaccharides are simple carbohydrates and are often called sugars.
2:20 - 3:05 | Monosaccharides
  • Monosaccharides are simple sugars.
  • Many monosaccharides follow the general formula CnH2nOn, where n is usually from 3 to 7.
  • They are classified by the number of carbon atoms they contain.
  • Pentose sugars such as ribose and deoxyribose have five carbon atoms.
  • Hexose sugars such as glucose, fructose, and galactose have six carbon atoms.
3:05 - 3:55 | Alpha and Beta Glucose
  • Glucose is a common example of a hexose sugar.
  • In glucose rings, carbon atoms are numbered starting from carbon 1 beside the oxygen and then moving clockwise.
  • Glucose has two forms: alpha glucose and beta glucose.
  • The two forms differ in the position of the hydroxyl group on carbon 1.
  • Alpha glucose has the hydroxyl group below carbon 1, while beta glucose has it above carbon 1.
3:55 - 4:35 | Key Properties of Glucose
  • Glucose is soluble in water because water molecules can form hydrogen bonds with its hydroxyl groups.
  • This solubility allows glucose to be transported easily in blood plasma.
  • Glucose is energy-rich because its bonds store energy.
  • Energy is released when these bonds are broken during respiration.
4:35 - 5:35 | Disaccharides and Condensation
  • Disaccharides form when two monosaccharides join together.
  • The joining reaction is called a condensation reaction.
  • A hydroxyl group from one monosaccharide reacts with a hydroxyl group from another monosaccharide.
  • This forms a covalent glycosidic bond and releases one molecule of water.
  • A glycosidic bond can form between different hydroxyl groups, not only between carbon 1 and carbon 4.
5:35 - 6:05 | Examples of Disaccharides
  • Maltose is made from two glucose molecules.
  • Sucrose is made from glucose and fructose.
  • Lactose is made from glucose and galactose.
6:05 - 6:35 | Hydrolysis of Disaccharides
  • Hydrolysis reactions break disaccharides down into monosaccharides.
  • Water is added during hydrolysis.
  • The added water breaks the glycosidic bond between the two monosaccharides.
